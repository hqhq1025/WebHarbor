Drug Interaction FAQs

What is a drug interaction?

A drug interaction occurs when a medication's effect is altered by another drug, food, beverage, or supplement. Interactions can make a medication less effective, cause unexpected side effects, or produce a new effect neither drug causes alone.

How do I use the drug interaction checker?

Type the name of a drug in the search box and click Add. Repeat for each medication, supplement, or substance you want to check. Once at least two drugs have been added, click Check Interactions to view the results.

What are the types of drug interactions?

Drug interactions fall into three main types: drug-drug (one medication affects another), drug-food (foods alter drug absorption or metabolism), and drug-disease (a drug worsens an existing medical condition). This checker covers drug-drug and drug-food/alcohol interactions.

What do the interaction severity ratings mean?

Major: The combination may be life-threatening or cause permanent damage. Avoid unless under close medical supervision. Moderate: The combination may worsen a medical condition or alter the effectiveness of treatment. Use cautiously with monitoring. Minor: The combination may result in increased frequency or severity of side effects, but generally will not require a major change in therapy.

Can I eat or drink grapefruit with my medicine?

Grapefruit and grapefruit juice can block an enzyme in your intestine that breaks down certain medications, causing higher-than-expected drug levels in your blood. Affected drugs include some statins (atorvastatin, simvastatin), some calcium channel blockers, some immunosuppressants, and others. Always check your medication guide or ask your pharmacist.

Can I drink alcohol while taking medications?

Alcohol can interact with many medications, including pain relievers (acetaminophen, NSAIDs), sleep aids, antidepressants, anxiety medications, antibiotics (metronidazole), diabetes medications, and blood thinners. Always check the label or ask your pharmacist before drinking alcohol with any prescription or OTC medication.

Can supplements interact with drugs?

Yes. Herbal supplements such as St. John's wort (reduces effectiveness of many drugs), ginkgo (increases bleeding risk), and garlic, as well as vitamins and minerals, can meaningfully alter the action of prescription drugs. Always tell your healthcare provider about all supplements you take.

Are drug interactions always dangerous?

No. Many interactions are minor and clinically insignificant. Some are even beneficial and are used intentionally in combination therapy. Your healthcare provider can help interpret what each interaction means for your specific situation.

What should I do if my drugs interact?

Do not stop taking any prescribed medication on your own. Contact your doctor or pharmacist promptly to discuss whether a dosage adjustment, timing change, or alternative therapy is appropriate.

Does this checker include all possible interactions?

No drug interaction database is complete. New interactions are discovered regularly. Always consult your pharmacist or doctor, especially if you add a new medication to your regimen. This tool is for informational purposes only and does not replace professional medical advice.
